Thoughts on Risk Factors for the day

Housing Prices and Pelegrini

At a 2007 hedge fund conference in Nassau, a Paulson & Co representative showed an interesting housing chart. Also in 2007, John Paulson became world-famous by shorting the US housing market by betting against mortgage backed securities through credit default swaps. His firm made $14Billion and Paulson himself made $4Billion on this one trade alone.

Currently, the rate of change in housing prices is falling and is mirrored in the share prices of the 3 homebuilding stocks in the S&P500: Lennar Corporation (LEN), D.R Horton (DHI) and NVR Inc (NVR). This is paralleled by monotonically increasing interest rates. In order to better represent the deceleration in housing prices, a chart similar to that developed by Paolo Pellegrini, the analyst who worked with Paulson, is shown below.

Mr. Pelegrini, used the inflation-adjusted growth in housing prices divided by wage growth. The chart below uses the year over year change in the seasonally adjusted housing price index divided by the inflation-adjusted hourly wage growth. It is interesting to note that the ratio slowed in April 2018 and has fallen close to its lowest point over the period displayed.

VaR as a Risk Measure

Value at Risk (VaR) is one of the most popular tools used in risk management and is incorporated within the Basel Accord for banking supervision. VaR is used by central counterparties (CCPs) for purposes of determining initial margin. VaR and Expected Shortfall (ES) are standard measures used by traders for setting risk limits and can be used by investors, traders and risk managers both for preventing panic selling (or buying) in periods of high volatility, and also preventing overreaching in periods of low volatility.

Our core solution is VaR.
